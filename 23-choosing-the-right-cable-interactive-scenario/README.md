# Choosing the right cable (interactive scenario)

THEORETICAL RECAP At OSI Layer 1 (Physical), the cable is not just a wire — it is a medium that determines speed, distance, interference resistance, and ultimately, the reliability of your entire network stack. Three main cable families dominate enterprise and lab environments: twisted pair copper (Cat5e, Cat6, Cat6a), coaxial, and fiber optic (single-mode vs multi-mode). Each has specific use cases tied to bandwidth requirements, distance constraints, and environmental conditions. A common beginner mistake is to treat all Ethernet cables as interchangeable. They are not. Cat5e supports up to 1 Gbps over 100m, while Cat6a reaches 10 Gbps over the same distance. Fiber optic eliminates electromagnetic interference entirely and can span kilometers — critical for inter-building links or data centers. Coaxial cable, while older, still appears in specific contexts such as cable internet or legacy CCTV systems. Another frequent error is ignoring the connector type and the directionality of the link: a straight-through cable connects different device types (PC to switch), while a crossover cable connects similar device types (switch to switch), though modern devices with Auto-MDI/MDIX largely handle this automatically. Understanding these distinctions is foundational for troubleshooting physical connectivity issues — a skill directly relevant to network reconnaissance in cybersecurity contexts. Mental model: think of the cable as the highway. The category or type defines the speed limit and maximum length.
